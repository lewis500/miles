Which of these two trades would save more gas?
  1. Trading a sedan that gets 28 mpg for a compact car that gets 38 mpg.
  2. Trading an S.U.V. that gets 15 mpg for a lighter one that gets 20 mpg.
Of course, a seems like the right answer, but let's check the math. According to the Federal Highway Administration, the average American drives about 13,500 miles per year. How many gallons per year will the average American save with each swap?

\[ \begin{aligned} \textbf{a.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{28 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{38 \, mpg} = 482 \, gal. - 355 \, gal. = 127 \, gal.\\ \textbf{b.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{15 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{20 \, mpg} = 900 \, gal. - 675 \, gal.= 225 \, gal.\\ \end{aligned} \] b saves nearly twice as much gas! If you call this wrong, you're not alone. A study published in Science by Richard Larrick and Jack Soll, professors at Duke's Fuqua School of Management, indicate that "there is a systematic misperception in judging fuel efficiency when it is expressed as miles per gallon." Namely, most people believe fuel usage declines linearly as a vehicle's miles per gallon rises, when the actual relationship is curved: an extra mile per gallon makes less of a difference on the consumption of efficient vehicle than to an inefficient one.
Now, you might think: "That's because going from 15 to 20 is a bigger percentage jump than going from 28 to 38!" But the misperception persists, even when you think proportionately.
