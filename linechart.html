Which of these two trades would save more gas?
  1. Trading a sedan that gets 28 mpg for a compact car that gets 38 mpg.
  2. Trading an S.U.V. that gets 15 mpg for a lighter one that gets 20 mpg.
Of course, a seems like the right answer. It's a 10 mpg increase, while b only bumps up the gas mileage by 5 mpg. But let's check the math. The average American drives about 13,500 miles per year. How many gallons per year will the average American save with each swap?

\[ \begin{aligned} \textbf{a.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{28 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{38 \, mpg} = 482 \, gal. - 355 \, gal. = 127 \, gal.\\ \textbf{b.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{15 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{20 \, mpg} = 900 \, gal. - 675 \, gal.= 225 \, gal.\\ \end{aligned} \] b saves nearly twice as much gas! It turns out that an extra mile per gallon makes less of a difference to efficient cars than to inefficient ones. Now, you might think:
That's because going from 15 to 20 is a bigger percentage jump than going from 28 to 38!
So let's try the choice again. Which of these two swaps would save more gallons of gasoline?
  1. Trading a sedan that gets 25 mpg for a hybrid that gets 50 mpg.
  2. Trading an old S.U.V. that gets 12 mpg for a newer one that gets 16 mpg.
No matter how you look at it, a seems like the right answer. In absolute terms, a's 25 mile per gallon increase is over six times larger than b's 4 mpg bump. In percentage terms, a represents a 100% increase in mpg, while b gets you just 25% more. To make sure, let's do the math. The average American drives about 13,500 miles per year. How many gallons per year will the average American save with each swap?

\[ \begin{aligned} \textbf{a.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{25 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{50 \, mpg} = 540 \, gal. - 270 \, gal. = 200 \, gal.\\ \textbf{b.} & \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{12 \, mpg} - \frac{13,500 \, mi.}{16 \, mpg} = 1,125 \, gal. - 844 \, gal.= 281 \, gal.\\ \end{aligned} \] It turns out that b saves 81 more gallons per year than a! How can this be? Well, a reduces gasoline consumption by half, and b reduces it by one third. But one half of a small number is less than one third of a large number.
